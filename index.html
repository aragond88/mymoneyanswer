How to decide what to do with your money

Most people know they should be doing something with their savings — but figuring out exactly what can feel overwhelming. Should you pay off debt or invest? Build an emergency fund or open a Roth IRA? The answer depends entirely on your personal financial situation, which is why generic advice rarely works.

This free tool walks you through a 4-question framework based on the same sequence used by financial advisors: assess your debt, check your emergency fund, understand your goals, and deploy your money in the right order. The sequence matters more than the amounts.

The right order of operations for your money

Financial experts broadly agree on a hierarchy: eliminate high-interest debt first, build a 3–6 month emergency fund in a high-yield savings account, maximize tax-advantaged accounts (Roth IRA, 401k), then invest additional savings in low-cost index funds. Skipping steps — investing while carrying 24% APR credit card debt, for example — is mathematically counterproductive regardless of how good the market is doing.

Frequently asked questions

What should I do with $1,000?
With $1,000, your priority depends on your situation. If you have high-interest debt, put it toward that first — it's the highest guaranteed return available. If you have no emergency fund, open a high-yield savings account. If your foundation is solid, open a Roth IRA and invest in a low-cost index fund like VTI. Use the tool above to get your specific plan.
Should I pay off debt or invest?
If your debt carries an interest rate above 8%, pay it off first — no investment reliably beats that guaranteed return. If your debt is below 7% (mortgage, student loans), investing while making minimum payments often makes mathematical sense, since long-term stock market returns average around 7–10% annually.
How much emergency fund do I need?
The standard recommendation is 3–6 months of living expenses in a high-yield savings account earning 4–5% APY. Start with a $1,000 starter emergency fund if you're also paying off debt, then build to 3 months, then 6 months. Self-employed people and single-income households should aim for 6+ months.
What is the best way to invest for beginners?
Open a Roth IRA (if eligible), then invest in a low-cost total market index fund like VTI or FSKAX. Expense ratios under 0.05% mean almost all of your return stays in your pocket. Automate monthly contributions and resist the urge to react to market fluctuations — time in market beats timing the market consistently.
What should I do with $10,000?
With $10,000 and no high-interest debt or emergency fund gaps: max your Roth IRA ($7,000 for 2025), put $2,000 in a HYSA as an emergency top-up, keep $1,000 liquid. If your Roth IRA is already maxed, open a taxable brokerage and invest in a total market index fund like VTI.
